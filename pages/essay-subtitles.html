Subtitles, Dubbing, and What Gets Lost

Essay · Silver Score, April 2026

Dubbing swaps an actor's instrument for a session performer reading on a headphone track. Subtitles ask you to do a bit of work — and give you back the performance the director actually shot.

What an actor's voice actually carries

Performance is half voice. Pitch, breath, the pause before a line, the way a word lands on a particular vowel in a particular language — none of that survives dubbing, even very good dubbing. A dubbed version of a film is a translation of the script, not a translation of the film.

Watch any scene from The Bridge in Swedish/Danish with subtitles and then in English dub. It's not the same show. Saga Norén's clipped Swedish cadence is the character.

Why the "distraction" complaint is backwards

The most common argument against subtitles is that you have to read them. In practice, after about fifteen minutes of any film, you stop reading them — you absorb them. Eye-tracking research has shown this for decades. What actually distracts is a mouth moving out of sync with the dub, or a delivery that doesn't match the face.

If anything, subtitles make you more present. You can't scroll your phone through a subtitled film; the audience that sticks with foreign-language TV is a more focused audience, and it shows in the writing.

The exception: animation and children's films

Dubbing is fine for animation, where voice is already a performance layered onto drawn faces, and for children's viewing where reading pace is the bottleneck. Spirited Away works beautifully in either mode. Live action is a different problem.

Where this changes our ratings

When we score a film or series, we assume you've watched it in its original language. Our 10 on The Bureau is the French 10. The dubbed version is a different object — we haven't rated it, and the people who seek it out are often the ones who bounce off the show entirely.

It's a small cost for a big gain: the performance the director actually approved, the rhythm the writer actually wrote, the world as the production designers actually built it.

Where to start

If subtitles are a new habit: try a short limited series first. The Chestnut Man (Danish, six episodes) is an easy entry. The Bureau is the long-term investment. Our full world-cinema pick-list: International Thrillers — Beyond English.
